Project 01 / 06

Agri-Tech
Farm Monitor

Course IDM05 — Independent Portfolio
Role Solo · End-to-end UX
Method Double Diamond
Platform Mobile (iOS / Android)
Year 2025 – 26

A remote-first farm monitoring app for part-time farmers — urban professionals who inherit or manage land alongside a full-time job. The design challenge was to compress complex agronomy into "a few focused minutes per day."

The User Problem

The user research surfaced a population that doesn't fit the traditional agritech persona: a 25-to-40-year-old urban professional managing inherited or secondary land. They suffer from time poverty — over 60-hour combined work weeks — and live at distance from the farm, relying on local workers for fragmented updates. They miss early warnings on pests, water stress, and soil health, which compound into yield loss and emotional stress.

Existing agritech apps are built for full-time, on-site experts: data-heavy dashboards and raw sensor outputs. For a time-poor, lower-expertise user, those interfaces actively work against the user's time budget. The brief: design against dashboard density.

Why Double Diamond

I chose the Double Diamond (Design Council, 2005) because the project required two distinct phases of divergence and convergence: first to understand a user group I wasn't part of, and second to shape a product without falling into the agritech-dashboard cliché. Jumping straight into screens would have produced a prettier version of the same wrong product.

Discover — Secondary Research

I synthesised peer-reviewed research on part-time farming (Song, Robinson & Bardsley, 2022; Scheyett et al., 2025; Ceriani et al., 2023) and grey-literature blogs from active farmers. Key insights: only 50% of part-time farmers report a viable work-life balance; the farm carries deep emotional and inherited value, so users persist despite financial loss; structural interventions — not motivation — are the missing piece.

Define — How Might We

Three HMW questions framed the convergent first diamond:

— How might we help time-poor farmers manage their farms in a few focused minutes a day?
— How might we provide trustworthy remote visibility when the user can't be on-site?
— How might we translate complex sensor and AI output into "do this today" guidance for non-experts?

Develop — Ideation Methods

I ran a 5 Whys exercise to push past symptoms (e.g. "the farm yields less") to root causes ("the user lacks time to interpret data, not lacks data"). I then used Crazy 8s to generate eight rapid sketches across the three core themes — water management, crop & pest health, and decision support — before reviewing for feasibility and user-fit.

Key UX Decisions

Remote-first information architecture. The home screen is a status overview — soil moisture, weather, pump state, pest risk — surfaced as glanceable indicators, not graphs. Anything that requires interpretation is hidden one tap deep.

AI as translator, not author. Sensor and AI outputs are converted into plain-language prompts ("irrigate Field B before 3pm — moisture at 18%, rain unlikely"). The user approves or delegates rather than reads dashboards. This is a deliberate move from raw data to interpreted recommendation — reducing cognitive load and protecting trust.

Cognitive load & familiar patterns. Mobile patterns (cards, tab bar, pull-to-refresh) were used deliberately rather than inventing novelty. For non-expert users under time pressure, familiarity is a feature.

Transparent rationale. Every recommendation surfaces a short "why" — which sensor, which threshold — so the user can build trust over time without being forced to learn agronomy.

Outcome & Forward Direction

The high-fidelity prototype prioritises irrigation as the lead module, with extension paths toward a "farm digital twin," predictive pest detection via drones and smart traps, and a long-term soil health score. The design justification rests on UX evidence that non-expert users benefit most from interpreted insight, transparent rationale, and familiar mobile patterns — not from richer dashboards.

Project 02 / 06

Safe Tech
Emergency Alert

Course IDM21 — Interaction Design
Role Solo · End-to-end UX
Method ISO 9241-210 HCD
Platform Mobile + Wearable
Year 2025

An offline-capable emergency alert and tracking system designed for low-connectivity scenarios — solo travel, remote regions, signal black-spots. Designed end-to-end through the ISO 9241-210 human-centred design cycle.

Why ISO 9241-210

For a safety-critical product the ISO HCD framework was the right scaffold. It enforces explicit phases — context-of-use, user requirements, design solution, evaluation — and treats the design as iterative rather than linear. For a product where misuse or false-activation has real consequences, this rigour matters more than speed.

User Context — Designing for Distress

The most important design constraint was the emotional state of the user at the moment of need. The user is panicked, possibly injured, possibly hidden, possibly silent. They cannot read instructions, choose between options, or recover from a wrong tap. The interface must therefore be designed for someone with fine motor difficulty, cognitive load, and high anxiety.

Literature also surfaced edge populations the design had to accommodate: hearing-impaired users for whom siren-based alerts fail; autistic users who find loud audio cues distressing; older adults who struggle with advanced settings; bystander-privacy concerns (Bowen-Forbes et al., 2024; Kummathi & Tejeswar, 2023; Rajaram et al., 2025).

Usability Requirements — Shneiderman's Five

I used Shneiderman's five usability attributes as the explicit acceptance criteria for the design:

Time to learn. A first-time user must be able to register, add an emergency contact, and arm the SOS in under five minutes — without reading a manual.
Speed of performance. SOS activation must be reachable in under two seconds — a single hold-to-trigger, with hardware-button and voice fallback.
Rate of errors. False activation is the dominant risk. A 5-second cancellable countdown and configurable trigger delay reduce it without slowing genuine activation.
Retention over time. The interface uses identical interaction patterns across screens — same SOS button shape, same colour, same placement — so muscle memory survives weeks of non-use.
Subjective satisfaction. A calm-but-urgent visual tone, immediate haptic feedback after every action, and reassuring language build trust during the rare moment the app is actually needed.

Task Modelling

I modelled three user tasks: set-up, emergency situation, and trigger SOS. The model branched on connectivity state — online (app trigger via internet), satellite (offline trigger via satellite link), and fully offline (LoRaWAN smart-pole relay or bystander-app SOS forwarding). Each branch terminates in a verifiable outcome — message delivered, location received, contact notified — rather than just a button-press.

Multimodal Triggers

A safety app cannot rely on the user being able to look at the screen. The design therefore includes four parallel trigger modes: in-app SOS button, hardware-button double-press, voice activation ("yell / shout" detection), and wearable double-press. Each is tuned to a different distress scenario — visible threat, hidden assault, vehicle collision, immobilising injury.

Accessibility as a First-Order Concern

High contrast, large tap targets (well above the 44pt baseline), redundant audio + haptic feedback, and short jargon-free copy. The wearable supports tactile vibration patterns so the device works for users who cannot hear or see the alert confirmation.

Outcome

The deliverable was a low-fidelity wireframe set, a clickable Figma prototype, and a documented design rationale traceable back to ISO 9241-210 phase outputs and Shneiderman's five attributes. Every screen choice — from the SOS button's central placement to the calming-but-urgent orange — is justified by a specific user-context constraint.

Project 03 / 06

Khan Academy
Usability Study

Course Design & Evaluation Project
Role Researcher & Designer
Method Mixed Methods Evaluation
Platform Mobile (iOS / Android)
Year 2025

A small-scale usability evaluation of the Khan Academy mobile app, combining qualitative and quantitative methods to surface friction in navigation, content search, and accessibility — followed by a justified redesign.

Why Mixed Methods

A single evaluation method gives a partial picture. Think-aloud exposes the cognitive process behind a task — what the user expected, where their mental model diverged from the system's. SUS gives a comparable, normalised perceived-usability score. Used together, they let me triangulate: I could see where users struggled (think-aloud) and how badly it felt overall (SUS). Either alone would have left blind spots.

Think-Aloud Protocol

I designed four tasks that exercised the app's most frequented pathways:

— Find a Career-related course (search & navigation).
— Open and start a lesson within a course (content depth).
— Return to dashboard and locate newly available courses (recovery and discovery).
— Find profile/account settings (system visibility).

Following Boren & Ramey (2000) I kept the moderator's voice minimal — neutral prompts only — to avoid biasing the user's verbalisation. Every hesitation, expectation gap, and recovery attempt was noted with a timestamp.

SUS — Quantifying the Subjective

John Brooke's System Usability Scale (1986) was applied immediately after each test session. Its alternating positive/negative phrasing reduces acquiescence bias, and its single normalised score lets me compare the existing app against the redesigned version on the same axis. Nielsen Norman Group describes SUS as the most robust standardised usability questionnaire for small samples (Laubheimer, 2018) — exactly the constraint here.

Formative vs Summative

Testing was conducted formatively — early enough that findings could feed back into design decisions, not just evaluate finished screens. Per the Nielsen Norman Group: "formative evaluations are diagnostic; they help fix problems while there's still time." A summative pass came later to verify that the redesign moved SUS in the right direction.

Sample & Justification

Five participants — students aged 18–30 — were chosen following the small-sample logic of Six & Macefield (2016): five users uncover roughly 80% of usability issues for any given task. The sample mixed expert app-users with first-time encounter users to capture both novice friction and experienced-user inefficiencies.

Findings → Redesign Decisions

Recurring friction clustered around three areas: search returned unexpected category collisions; the navigation back to dashboard was inconsistent across screens; and account settings were buried under non-obvious icon choices. The redesigned wireframes addressed each: clearer search filters with visible category scoping, a persistent dashboard anchor in the tab bar, and an explicit "Profile" label rather than an avatar-only icon (per Nielsen's heuristic on visibility of system status and recognition over recall).

Outcome

A redesigned interface backed by a quantitative SUS uplift and qualitative confirmation that the previously failed tasks were now completed without hesitation. Critically, every redesign decision is traceable to a specific finding — there are no "improvements" without evidence.

Project 04 / 06

Scrum Tool
Redesign

Course ISM105 — Business Systems
Role Solo · UI & UX
Method UX Laws & Visual Principles
Platform Web (Desktop)
Year 2025

A redesign of a Scrum management tool — Product Backlog, Sprint Backlog, and Burndown Chart — applied as a deliberate exercise in invoking UX laws, Gestalt principles, and Bertin's visual variables, rather than as a free-form aesthetic redesign.

Why Constrain to Principles

Scrum tools accumulate features and become cognitively expensive. Rather than intuiting fixes, I treated the redesign as a structured application of codified design knowledge — UX laws, Gestalt grouping, and Bertin's visual variables. The constraint forced every decision to be defensible, not stylistic.

UX Laws Applied

Jakob's Law — users spend most of their time on other apps, so familiar Kanban patterns (To Do, In Progress, Done) were preserved, not redesigned. The existing mental model is the asset.

Fitts's Law — frequently-tapped targets (Add Task, Sprint switching) were enlarged and placed near natural cursor anchors. Destructive actions were intentionally smaller and farther.

Hick's Law — top-level choices were reduced. The original tool exposed every option at every level; the redesign hides secondary controls under progressive disclosure so first-time users aren't paralysed.

Tesler's Law of Conservation of Complexity — Scrum has irreducible complexity (priority, sprint mapping, status, owner, due date, files). I made an explicit choice about who handles which complexity: the system formats and groups it, the user only chooses meaningful values. Over-simplification would have stripped useful information; the law forced an honest trade-off.

Gestalt Principles for Grouping

Proximity — navigation and search were spaced apart enough to read as separate functional groups without needing extra borders or boxes.

Similarity — buttons of equivalent function share shape, colour, and size; cards share an internal layout. Users learn the pattern once and reapply it everywhere.

Common Region — sprint columns are enclosed in soft container shapes so each sprint reads as its own unit, even when columns are scrolled adjacent. The shape does the grouping work, not the spacing alone.

Bertin's Visual Variables for Hierarchy

Size distinguishes headlines from sub-headings, and primary buttons (Add, Start) from secondary ones — encoding hierarchy without colour-only signalling. Colour is used as a status channel only (priority red/amber/green, status pill), never for decoration. Shape separates interactive containers from static information. The result: the eye can locate priority, status, and action at a glance, which is exactly the workflow Scrum demands.

Core UX Principles

Consistency across cards, buttons, and column structure. Hierarchy through type-size and weight rather than colour-saturation. User control — task cards are reorderable and editable inline, so the user feels they own the workflow rather than being walked through it.

Outcome

The redesigned Product Backlog, Sprint Backlog, and Burndown Chart screens reduce visual noise without removing essential information. Every change traces to a named law or principle, which made the design defensible in critique and easy to extend without breaking internal logic.

Project 05 / 06

Wanted:
Billy the Bookworm

Course IDM04 — Design Thinking
Role Team of 5 · UX, Research, Build
Method Design Thinking (5-phase)
Platform Hybrid — Physical + Web
Year 2025

A hybrid physical-digital escape room that teaches University of Brighton students how to use the Aldrich Library — Dewey Decimal navigation, citation, self-service kiosks — through a story-driven case file rather than a tutorial.

The Real Problem

User research surfaced an uncomfortable truth: students have a library they don't know how to use. Three of five interviewed students struggled with both physical and digital navigation; three preferred the help desk over self-service tools; two could not differentiate between resource types. The library wasn't the problem — its discoverability was.

Why Design Thinking

Brown's (2009) five-phase Design Thinking model — Empathise, Define, Ideate, Prototype, Test — was the right shape for an open, exploratory brief. The team didn't yet know whether the solution should be an app, a poster campaign, a tour, or something else; the divergent ideation phase was load-bearing here in a way it wouldn't be on a more constrained brief.

Empathise — Primary & Secondary Research

Five participants from the user pool, semi-structured interviews following the IDEO (2015) human-centred design field guide. We built affinity diagrams from the transcripts to surface pattern-clusters rather than individual quotes. In parallel, we benchmarked competitor escape rooms (UNT, UNM, ASU) and mapped their gaps — most taught navigation but missed citation; some taught citation but missed physical-library fluency. The gap we filled was both, in one experience.

Define — From Pain Points to Goals

We translated each user pain point into a learning goal: navigation friction → "learn the Dewey Decimal system"; help-desk dependence → "use self-service kiosks confidently"; citation anxiety → "use Cite Them Right correctly." Each goal then became a puzzle in the escape room's narrative.

Ideate — Mind & Concept Mapping

A mind map of the library system surfaced touchpoints where confusion most often occurs. A concept map shaped the structure of the experience itself — narrative flow, role of player, rules, and required physical/digital resources. Together these tools translated user pain into game mechanics.

Why Hybrid (Not Pure Digital)

Pure digital would have failed the brief — students need to learn the physical library too. Pure physical would have lost the validation loop that escape rooms need (you must know when a puzzle is solved). The hybrid case file lets puzzles live on paper while solutions are validated on a web app — physical engagement, digital feedback, both reinforced.

Gamification & Constructive Learning

Per Deterding et al. (2011) and Veldkamp et al. (2020), gamification works when learning outcomes are embedded in the game mechanics, not bolted on as quizzes. A puzzle that requires you to look up a Dewey number to find a clue is a Dewey-number-learning task disguised as a clue-hunt — exactly the constructive-learning sweet spot.

Prototype & Test

We started with a paper-based low-fidelity case file and a stripped-down digital companion (first three sections only) so we could test puzzle logic before committing to visual polish. Iterations were tested with users using think-aloud and SUS, with severity ratings on issues (Nielsen, 1994) so we knew what to fix first.

Outcome

A working hybrid escape-room experience — printed case file with embedded clues, plus a React-based web companion that validates solutions, advances the narrative, and ends with a measurable learning outcome. Students leave knowing how to use the library, having had fun doing it.

Project 06 / 06

Ethics in AI-Driven
Spatial Design

Course IDM25 — Research Project
Role Co-author · Literature review
Method Systematic Review (PRISMA)
Format Peer-format Research Paper
Year 2025 – 26

A peer-format systematic literature review investigating how AI reshapes ethical concerns in XR, VR, AR, and metaverse environments — and what UX practice will need to look like when "the interface" is the user's body, gaze, and surrounding space.

Why This Matters for UX

Spatial design breaks several assumptions that screen-based UX takes for granted. Consent becomes ambient — sensors collect behaviour from bystanders who never agreed to participate. Anonymity collapses — motion and gaze data alone are sufficient to identify a user, even without name or face. Persuasion intensifies — embodied immersion makes manipulation feel like memory. UX research that ignores these shifts will be designing on top of foundations that no longer hold.

Method — PRISMA Systematic Review

We followed the PRISMA framework (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses) over peer-reviewed papers published 2019–2025. The framework forced explicit inclusion criteria, search-string transparency, and screening reproducibility — turning what could have been an opinion essay into a defensible evidence synthesis. Thematic mapping was then used to identify gaps across sociological, psychological, and design perspectives.

Six Ethical Themes Identified

Privacy & Surveillance — gaze, gesture, and behavioural data are uniquely identifying. Traditional consent models fail in continuous-sensing environments.
Transparency & Explainability — users can't see how AI interprets their movements or why the system responds as it does.
Fairness & Inclusion — biased training data produces unequal experiences across demographic and ability groups.
Autonomy & Manipulation — "immersive nudging" blurs the line between guidance and manipulation in ways the user cannot easily detect.
Governance & Accountability — current XR is governed largely by industry self-regulation; legal frameworks lag.
Sustainability — energy use, device lifecycle, and e-waste are largely absent from spatial UX research, despite obvious environmental footprint.

The Implementation Gap

The review's central finding is what we called the implementation gap: the literature broadly agrees on principles — privacy, fairness, transparency, autonomy, accountability — but most frameworks remain aspirational. Concrete tools, standards, and governance mechanisms for XR are still nascent. Designers know what good looks like; they don't yet have the toolkit to deliver it.

What This Means for Spatial UX Practice

The paper recommends future work on (a) practical toolkits that operationalise ethical principles in concrete XR design decisions; (b) dynamic, context-aware consent models for continuous and bystander sensing; (c) privacy-preserving techniques for motion and gaze data evaluated against usability and immersion; (d) inclusive co-design with disabled users where accessibility is treated as a fairness intervention rather than a feature add-on; and (e) integration of environmental metrics into spatial UX research itself.

Why This Project

Including a research paper alongside applied design work is intentional. UX is not only about screens — it's about understanding the conditions under which interfaces operate. Spatial design is where the next decade of UX will be tested, and being able to read, synthesise, and contribute to peer literature is part of being a designer rather than just a screen-maker.
